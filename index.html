01The Problem

A platform serving 190+ services had no reliable way to learn if those services were working

Canada's federal transportation sector runs over 190 citizen-facing services. But the agency's digital platform, oneTC, had a critical blind spot: its client feedback mechanism was fragmented, inconsistent, and not being used to drive improvement decisions.

→Feedback collected in isolated pockets — no shared taxonomy, no synthesis loop connecting frontline input to program leads
→No common standard for what 'good feedback' even looked like across 190+ service lines
→Service teams making design decisions in the dark, without access to collected user signal
→GCKey and myTC operated independently — users had to create accounts twice with no linking mechanism

"Services require direct enrollment through the service team in the present state — there's no unified account management and no way to connect what one service knows about a user to another service they're trying to access."

— Synthesized from cross-team working group session, November 2023

02The Research

Understanding the landscape through both scale and depth

The core research question wasn't just 'what's broken' — it was 'what's systemically broken across hundreds of services and dozens of teams.' That required a mixed-methods approach from the start.

📊

Mapped the full landscape of 190+ TC services — cataloguing how client feedback was currently collected, stored, and acted upon. Revealed that most services had no formalized feedback loop at all.
🎨

Facilitated sessions with cross-functional TC employees (program officers, service leads, IT architects, policy staff). Active design workshops — not passive interviews — with wireframes and workflow diagrams captured live on Miro.
🔍

Examined how federal departments across Canada and internationally handled client feedback — including GCKey's MFA rollout, IRCC's standardized registration, and Canada's Digital Standards.

Cross-organization comparison matrix analyzing feedback mechanisms, account management standards, and synthesis approaches across Canadian federal departments and international examples.
🗺️

👤
Persona Creation
Developed user personas representing service staff, program managers, and external clients — grounding the recommendations in real-world use cases and friction points across different roles.

Key Findings from the Scan
01
Federal standards exist — TC isn't using them
GCKey Level 3 MFA, IRCC's registration field standards, and the Alpha Design System at digital.canada.ca all provide ready-to-adopt patterns. TC's current state is behind existing federal standards, not just industry benchmarks.
Source: Working Group Nov 14 · Session 5 (Alpha Design System reference)
02
The bank model is the user's mental model
Workshop participants and users consistently referenced banking as their expectation benchmark — single login, personalized dashboard, account recovery via notification. TC is competing against this expectation with a fragmented experience.
Source: Session 1 (login partner reference) · Session 7 (Intuit workflow reference)
03
Mode-based routing is the structural fix
Both GOV.UK and the bank model route users by need/role at entry — not by presenting every service simultaneously. TC's proposed mode selection (Civil Aviation / Marine / Rail / Personal / Business) directly mirrors this proven pattern.
Source: Sessions 2, 4, 5 · Working Group Nov 14

03The Decisions

From fragmented findings to a coherent design direction

The research surfaced a system built for data collection — not for learning or service improvement. Three core design decisions shaped the strategic direction.

1
Separate feedback collection from feedback synthesis
Rather than proposing a new collection tool, the priority was creating a synthesis layer — a structured process by which collected feedback would be routed to the right people at the right cadence. This decision was grounded in the finding that data existed but wasn't being used.
2
Standardize feedback taxonomy across service lines
The comparative analysis revealed that inconsistent categorization was the root cause of why feedback couldn't be aggregated. A shared taxonomy — even a simple one — would allow cross-service pattern recognition for the first time. This was the lowest-effort, highest-impact structural change identified.
3
Recommend role-based feedback routing, not just collection
Workshop sessions revealed that program leads were not receiving the feedback being collected — it was sitting with frontline teams who had no mechanism to escalate it. The recommendation was to create explicit role definitions (who collects, who synthesizes, who acts) rather than assume feedback would find its way up organically.

"How can we update the name on the portal if someone gets married? How do we handle account recovery when users forget credentials and create duplicates? These aren't edge cases — they're high-frequency problems that need to be prioritized before edge cases."

— Session participant (Nov 2023), reinforcing the need for systemic design over one-off fixes

↗Metrics Framework

Measuring the metrics that matter

Applied from the Service Design: Measuring the Metrics that Matter framework — grounding the TC recommendations in concepts of Time Tax, Failure Demand, and the Effectiveness vs. Efficiency split to ensure we targeted real service improvement, not vanity tracking.

Core Framework

Effectiveness vs. Efficiency

Before recommending any measurement approach for oneTC, the recommendations were framed around two distinct questions — applied at different phases of the project cycle.

🎯"Are we building the right thing?"

Service Effectiveness · Early cycle / problem definition

  • Does the feedback mechanism actually improve citizen services — or just collect data?
  • Are service teams acting on the feedback collected, not just storing it?
  • Is the right signal reaching the right decision-makers at the right cadence?
  • Measured through: success rate achieving expected outcomes for users, staff, and the organization
⚙️"Are we building it right?"

Service Efficiency · Delivery phase

  • How much time does feedback take to travel from collection to a decision?
  • Are duplicate accounts and credential failures being resolved without manual escalation?
  • Is administrative burden being reduced across service enrollment flows?
  • Measured through: processing time, support ticket volume, user satisfaction signal

Key Concepts Applied to TC

⏱️
Time Tax
Wasted time in service processes
  • ·Understanding complex eligibility requirements and forms
  • ·Waiting on hold or navigating a confusing website to find the right service
  • ·Coordinating between departments just to achieve a single outcome
  • ·Correcting errors from duplicate account creation and credential loss
TC context: Maya's journey required 3 separate logins and a phone call to complete a single service request.
🔁
Failure Demand
Upstream failures creating downstream work
  • ·80% of Nova Scotia heating rebate calls came from failed online transactions — not genuine service needs
  • ·~60% of Alberta court contact centre calls were avoidable — ~$500k annual savings potential
  • ·TC parallel: support calls driven by no self-serve recovery, no status tracking, and no routing confirmation after form submission
Reducing failure demand was reframed as a cost-reduction argument, not just a UX one — strengthening the executive case.

Surface metrics measure symptoms. The recommendations targeted the hidden layers — process, governance, and people — that cause the visible failures.

Service Metrics Cards — Applied to TC

Effectiveness Card
Service job to be done
Enable TC service teams to collect, synthesize, and act on citizen feedback to continuously improve the 190+ services on oneTC.
Outcomes: % of services with active feedback loops · % of feedback routed to program leads · decision lag reduction
Efficiency Card
Administrative burden to reduce
Reduce the manual effort caused by duplicate accounts, unstructured feedback, and lack of routing — freeing service teams to focus on improvement rather than triage.
Measures: duplicate account rate · support call volume · time-to-synthesis per feedback item
💡

KPI Discipline: Recommendations were limited to 2–3 core metrics per service to avoid vanity metrics that track volume without impact. Metrics were co-created with stakeholders, users, and internal staff — using their language, not design jargon. The sequencing mattered too: start with efficiency improvements (standardized taxonomy, defined routing roles) to build trust before tackling effectiveness-level questions about whether the right services are being built at all.

04The Solution

A strategic recommendations report built for executive decision-making

The deliverable combined current-state findings, comparative analysis, and a prioritized roadmap — designed specifically to be actionable at the executive level, not just informative.

Short-termImmediate Fixes
  • 1Conduct targeted user research with both internal TC employees and external service users to validate pain points before committing to solutions
  • 2Establish a shared feedback taxonomy across all TC services as an immediate structural fix
  • 3Define explicit roles for feedback collection, synthesis, and escalation within each service team
  • 4Prioritize high-frequency account issues (credential recovery, duplicate accounts) before addressing edge cases
Long-termStrategic Direction
  • 5Create a new TC-wide strategy for how client feedback will be managed across the department — covering roles, feedback methods, and data management
  • 6Identify and standardize feedback channels across the platform — including in-app prompts, chatbot integration, and post-service surveys

↺Reflection

What I'd do differently

Push for external user research earlier

Several workshop participants noted that the proposed workflows were designed from an internal perspective and needed validation with the citizens actually using TC's services. "Whatever order the modes come up with, it should be based on talking to not only internal but external users as well." — this feedback arrived late, and earlier external research would have strengthened the recommendations significantly.

Pilot before recommending at scale

I'd have advocated for a lightweight pilot — testing the new feedback taxonomy on one or two services before recommending a department-wide rollout. In government contexts, evidence from a small-scale pilot carries more weight in executive decision-making than comparative analysis alone. A pilot would also have surfaced implementation friction before it became a scaling problem.
