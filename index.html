01The Problem

190+ citizen services. One person reviewing feedback manually. Zero loop back into the system.

oneTC is Transport Canada's whole-of-government digital transformation initiative - the platform meant to unify 190+ citizen-facing services under one coherent experience. But before TC could make those services better, it had a more fundamental problem: it had no reliable way to hear what citizens thought about them.

→The feedback bottleneck: All citizen feedback across 190+ services funnelled to one team member who manually reviewed tickets with no triage system, no taxonomy, and no defined escalation path. Feedback sat - or was discarded.
→Buried access points: My navigation analysis showed users needed 4-6 clicks minimum to reach a contact method - and 67 services offered only a generic footer link, no email or phone at all.
→Inconsistent channels: 73 services had an email, 60 had a phone number, but most had different contacts per service - no standard. Some social media links in the footer were broken entirely.
→No implementation path: Even when feedback was collected, there was no system to ingest it into active sprints or influence design decisions department-wide. The feedback loop was broken at every stage.

"TC's current disjointed feedback strategy can cause clients to have negative biases toward the department - and those biases compound over time when experiences don't improve."

02The Research

Four methods. One question: how does a citizen actually reach Transport Canada - and does TC even hear them?

This wasn't a research project about what to build. It was about diagnosing a system before recommending how to fix it. I needed to understand every step a citizen took just to leave feedback - and what happened (or didn't) once they did.

🗺️
Navigation Click-Path Analysis
For each of the 190+ services, I documented the full navigation path a user had to take to reach any form of contact - tracing every click from Canada.ca home to the point of feedback. Most required 4-6 steps; some services had no contact method at all above the footer. All findings captured in Excel.
📊
Service Landscape Audit
Catalogued all 190+ services by feedback method: email present (73), phone number present (60), footer contact only (67), no contact mentioned. Identified regional inconsistencies - different contact methods for the same service depending on province - and confirmed 5 shared social media channels with several broken links.
🌐
Environmental Scan
Researched how comparable organizations handle feedback collection - including US healthcare.gov (direct "Connect with Us" in footer), UK GOV.UK (structured categorized feedback per service), and Canadian banks (RBC, TD) which use in-app prompts and post-service micro-surveys. Also studied how other Canadian federal departments (IRCC, CRA) manage feedback channels.
⚖️
SWOT Analysis
Synthesized a full SWOT specifically for TC's feedback ecosystem - grounding each point in the audit data. Documented on a Miro board along with a cognitive bias analysis: identifying which biases (information bias, negativity bias, sunk cost fallacy) TC's broken feedback system was actively triggering in citizens and how to design against them.
🧠
Cognitive Bias Mapping
A unique layer of the research: mapped three cognitive biases that TC's current fragmented strategy was creating - Information Bias (users seeking info that can't affect outcomes), Negativity Bias (bad experiences leaving bigger impressions than good ones), and Sunk Cost Fallacy (resources invested in channels that produce no useful signal). Documented on Miro with design implications for each.
📋
Recommendations Deck for Executive Director
Consolidated all findings into a structured presentation delivered directly to the Executive Director. The deck translated audit data into short-term quick wins and a long-term TC-wide feedback strategy. The recommendations were approved and fed into the oneTC SDSC roadmap for June 2024 sprint planning.

The deeper problem: feedback collected, but never actioned
Even when a citizen successfully navigated to a contact method and submitted feedback, it went into a black hole. One team member at TC was responsible for manually reviewing all incoming tickets across all services - with no triage logic, no taxonomy to categorize by service or issue type, no escalation path, and no mechanism to route insights back to program leads. The "feedback loop" ended at inbox receipt.
What I observed
→Not all service pages have any contact email or phone number
→Not all service pages encourage the reader to leave feedback
→There are different ways to leave feedback by region - no national standard
→Many services have different contacts for each sub-service within them
→Some social media links in the footer are broken
→No feedback link on the TC home page - only visible after selecting a certification path
Cognitive impact on citizens
Information Bias
Citizens seek additional information even when it can't change their outcome - poor service pages create unnecessary anxiety and calls to already-overloaded centres
Negativity Bias
Negative experiences leave a stronger cognitive imprint than positive ones - a broken link or dead-end navigation creates lasting distrust in TC services
Sunk Cost Fallacy
Resources invested in channels that produce no useful signal - TC continues operating feedback channels that no one is monitoring or acting on

Key Findings from the Scan
01
US healthcare.gov shows the quick win
healthcare.gov added a "Connect with Us" block in the footer with phone, local help, blog, and social links - all visible on every page with zero navigation required. TC could implement this immediately on tc.canada.ca. The language is encouraging, not passive. This became Recommendation 2 in my ED presentation.
Source: Environmental Scan · healthcare.gov footer analysis
02
GOV.UK and the banks close the loop - TC doesn't
GOV.UK routes structured feedback by service to program owners who review quarterly. Canadian banks surface real-time status to submitters. TC's current state stops at inbox - no synthesis, no routing, no visibility back to the citizen. The gap isn't just access; it's the entire back-end of feedback.
Source: Environmental Scan · GOV.UK service feedback documentation · Bank in-app feedback comparison
03
In-app prompts are the long-term answer
Both GOV.UK's inline prompt ("Was this page helpful?") and Canadian banking apps' persistent feedback button demonstrate that feedback works best when it's zero-navigation and context-aware. This informed the long-term recommendation: a standardized in-app feedback mechanism embedded in the oneTC portal, not linked from the footer.

03Key Findings

What the data actually showed - beyond the numbers

The audit produced quantitative findings, but the more important work was translating those numbers into a clear picture of the system's failure mode and what it would take to fix it.

1
The feedback system wasn't broken - it was never built
TC had collected feedback mechanisms (email addresses, phone numbers, a footer Contact Us link) but had not built a feedback system. There was no taxonomy, no routing logic, no synthesis process, and no defined path from citizen input to program decision. One team member manually reviewing inbound tickets was not a system - it was a workaround that created the illusion of a feedback loop without the substance of one.
2
Navigation depth was the first barrier - and most citizens never got past it
Requiring 4-6 clicks before a citizen could even find a contact method meant that only the most motivated users were providing feedback. The 67 services with footer-only contact had effectively opted out of feedback entirely - not by policy, but by design neglect. The first recommendation wasn't about the feedback form itself; it was about surfacing the front door.
3
Inconsistency was more damaging than absence
A user navigating three related civil aviation services would encounter three different email addresses, potentially two different phone numbers, and in some cases no contact at all - even within the same certification area. This inconsistency was more cognitively damaging than having no contact at all, because it signalled organizational disorganization and undermined trust in whether anyone was actually listening.
4
The language on TC's pages didn't invite feedback - it just didn't block it
None of the 190+ service pages actively encouraged citizens to provide feedback. The wording was passive at best - "Contact Us" at the footer, with no framing about why it mattered or what TC would do with it. Comparatively, healthcare.gov says "Connect with Us" and lists channels with clear labels. GOV.UK says "Was this page helpful?" with a one-tap response. The bar for encouraging feedback is not high - TC just hadn't cleared it.

04Recommendations

Short-term fixes. Long-term strategy. One approved direction.

The recommendations were structured in two horizons: quick wins that required no new infrastructure and could be implemented in active sprints, and a long-term strategic direction for how TC should design its feedback ecosystem going forward.

Strategic Framework
The oneTC Feedback Strategy
Approved by the Executive Director of Transport Canada's Digital Services branch and fed into the June 2024 SDSC roadmap. The framework separates what TC should do now from what it should build toward.
Short-term recommendations
Quick wins · Active sprints
  • Perform further environmental scan with other Canadian federal departments to understand how they manage feedback - build from existing evidence
  • Identify current-state roles and responsibilities for feedback intake and complete a stakeholder map - the single-reviewer problem needs immediate structural fix
  • Identify a list of "quick wins" - starting with: rewriting page copy to actively encourage feedback, adding a direct contact link on the TC main page, and re-verifying and repairing all social media links in the footer
Long-term recommendations
Strategic direction · Future sprints
  • Create a TC-wide strategy for how client feedback will be managed across the department - covering roles, feedback methods, data management, synthesis, and escalation
  • Identify and standardize feedback channels across all services - exploring in-app prompts, chatbot integration, and post-service surveys as the primary mechanisms (SurveyMonkey or Typeform as candidate tools, subject to GC data compliance review)
  • Define how citizen feedback will be inculcated into active design sprints - so that feedback doesn't end at inbox receipt but routes to program leads and drives measurable service improvement

↺Reflection

What I'd do differently

Run a pilot before recommending at scale

The TSC and Civil Aviation pilots were already running, but I would have advocated to pause and learn from them explicitly before finalizing the long-term recommendations. In government contexts, evidence from a small-scale pilot carries significantly more weight in executive decision-making than comparative analysis alone. A structured pilot readout would have made the recommendations even harder to push back on - and surfaced implementation friction before it became a scaling problem.

Bring citizens into the research earlier

The audit was comprehensive, but it was built from an internal vantage point - what TC's own pages offered citizens, not what citizens actually experienced. Earlier qualitative research with actual service users (rather than program staff) would have surfaced the emotional dimension of navigating 6 clicks to a dead-end contact form in a way that the Excel data alone couldn't capture. The cognitive bias framework helped, but lived experience would have been more powerful in the ED presentation.
